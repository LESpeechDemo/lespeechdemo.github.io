1. Abstract

Cross-language speaker-adaptive text-to-speech (TTS) has advanced rapidly, enabling more controllable speech generation across different languages. However, existing approaches suffer from limited language coverage, with state-of-the-art systems supporting only 0.5\% of the world’s 7,000+ languages. One common method to expand language coverage is to pre-train the model on a large number of languages upfront. However, this requires collecting extensive multilingual data all at once, which is particularly challenging for many low-resource languages. An alternative approach is to pre-train a model on several languages and then fine-tune it for a new language, a process that may lead to catastrophic forgetting, thereby degrading performance on previously learned languages. To address these challenges, we explore a third approach and propose Language Expert Speech (LESpeech), a cross-language speaker-adaptive TTS framework that seamlessly integrates unseen low-resource languages without fine-tuning the pre-trained modules. By disentangling speech generation into two phases, LESpeech enables efficient language expansion post-training by training a new language expert for the target language using just one hour of parallel audio-text data. Our experiments demonstrate that LESpeech achieves competitive speech quality compared to models fully pre-trained in the target language.

4. Conclusion

LESpeech is the first cross-language speaker-adaptive TTS which provides a unrestricted language scalable solution. By fully disentangling the language generation process into two phases, LESpeech eliminates the need for fine-tuning pre-trained modules when extending to new languages, combining the flexibility of zero-shot speaker adaptation with an efficient mechanism for language expansion. Thus, LESpeech can continuously extend its language coverage without compromising synthesis quality. This approach improves access to controllable, high-quality TTS for low-resource languages and fosters further research and development in the speech research community.
